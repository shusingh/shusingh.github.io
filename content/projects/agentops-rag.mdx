## Why I built it

Most RAG demos prove that retrieval can work once. AgentOps RAG asks a more useful question: what does the system look like when retrieval fails, ingestion breaks, tenants must be isolated, answers need citations, and expensive endpoints need operational controls?

The goal was to build a portfolio-grade AI infrastructure project that shows the production surface area around agentic retrieval. It is intentionally not a chatbot-first demo. The useful part is the discipline around the agent.

## What it includes

- **Tenant isolation** with JWT-derived tenant IDs at API and retrieval boundaries.
- **Async ingestion** through Redis Streams, with worker processing and DLQ replay.
- **Hybrid retrieval** using BM25 and vector search in OpenSearch.
- **Explicit agent runtime** with planner, retriever, critic, and finalizer stages.
- **Citation-grounded answers** with evidence-based refusals when support is missing.
- **Eval harness** with JSONL cases and generated reports.
- **OpenTelemetry traces** across auth, ingestion, retrieval, model calls, critic checks, and finalization.
- **Redis Lua rate limiting** for tenant and endpoint-level control.
- **Benchmark reports** for throughput, latency, failures, refusals, and rate-limit blocks.

## Architecture

The runtime is explicit Python orchestration rather than a framework-hidden graph. That makes the infrastructure decisions visible:

1. The planner decides whether to retrieve, refuse, or ask for clarification.
2. The retriever runs tenant-filtered hybrid search.
3. The critic checks citation coverage and answer support.
4. The finalizer returns either a grounded answer or a refusal.

The ingestion path is similarly explicit. Uploads become queued jobs, workers chunk and index content, metadata lands in Postgres, and failures go to a DLQ with tenant ID, document ID, stage, and retry count.

## Why it matters

This project is valuable because it demonstrates the parts of AI systems that senior engineering teams actually care about:

- Can the system fail visibly instead of returning unsupported answers?
- Can a tenant ever retrieve another tenant's documents?
- Can an operator replay failed ingestion jobs without rerunning everything?
- Can evals catch regressions before they hit users?
- Can traces explain why an answer was refused or why retrieval missed?
- Can rate limits degrade differently for read-heavy and write-heavy paths?

Those are the questions that separate a useful AI platform from a demo that looks good on a laptop.

## What I would do next

The natural next step is to harden it like a real deployment: managed Postgres, Redis, and OpenSearch; Alembic migrations; OTLP trace export; retrieval drift monitoring; offline eval gates in CI; worker autoscaling; and stronger audit logs around tenant onboarding and replay actions.
